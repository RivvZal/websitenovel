Chapter 2: Library Echoes

After that day, I began noticing her. Maybe she was always there, or maybe life had finally decided to give me something worth holding onto.
She always sat by the window in the library during lunch, her notebook open, sometimes sketching, sometimes scribbling poetry in a language I could never fully decipher from afar.
I started going to the library too. At first, I told myself it was just for the peace and quiet. But the truth was simple: I wanted to see her again.
We exchanged glances. Small smiles. And then, finally, words.

"The Fault in Our Stars?" she asked one day, tilting her head.

I looked down at the book in my hands. “Yeah.”

“I’ve read it three times. It hurts. But it’s honest.”

I nodded slowly. “I like it because it doesn’t lie. It tells you straight up—some stories just aren’t meant to end happily.”

She looked at me, and for a second, her expression softened into something fragile.

“Real stories rarely do,” she whispered.

That was the beginning of our daily meetings. We never planned them, but somehow, we always ended up in the same corner of the library. She would draw while I read. Sometimes we’d talk, sometimes not. But it never felt awkward. It just felt right.
She showed me her sketches once. They were beautiful. Melancholic. Lonely.

“Why don’t you draw faces?” I asked.

“I don’t know what they look like,” she answered. “Not really. But I know how they feel.”

Every day I was with her, I forgot about the clock ticking inside me. I forgot that my life had an expiration date. All I could think about was her—and how being around her made me feel alive.
